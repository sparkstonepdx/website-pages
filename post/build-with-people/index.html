Build With People, Not In Isolation

A lot of us want to help. We want to build good software, useful tools, things that matter. There’s a strong urge to retreat into making “the right thing” — to design something elegant, principled, and technically solid as a response to everything feeling chaotic.

Things feel pretty intense right now.

A lot of us want to help. We want to build good software, useful tools, things that matter. There’s a strong urge to retreat into making “the right thing” — to design something elegant, principled, and technically solid as a response to everything feeling chaotic.

But there’s a trap hiding in that instinct.

If you build in isolation, you’re guessing. And no matter how well-intentioned or well-engineered something is, guessing is a poor substitute for working directly with people who actually need help.

The part that stuck with me

Recently I was watching a Linus Tech Tips video where Linus Torvalds was on, watching “lesser Linus” build a computer for him. At one point, he said something that really stuck.

He mentioned that he doesn’t actually care how popular Linux is. He’s not chasing adoption numbers or mindshare.

But then he added something important: users matter.

Not because popularity is the goal — but because without users, the work stops making sense. If no one were using Linux, there’d be no reason to keep building it. It wouldn’t matter how correct or elegant or well-designed it was. Without users, it would just be time spent in a vacuum.

That framing feels especially relevant right now.

Perfection doesn’t create value on its own

It’s tempting to believe that if we just build the best version of a thing — the cleanest architecture, the most principled approach — people will eventually find it.

Sometimes that happens. Often it doesn’t.

A “perfect” tool that no one uses doesn’t help anyone. And worse, it can quietly drain your energy while convincing you that you’re making progress.

Real value only shows up when your work connects with actual people and actual needs.

Community comes first

The most reliable way I’ve found to build something useful is also the least glamorous:

  • talk to people
  • listen more than you explain
  • work alongside them
  • build small things together

When you do that, the work changes. Your assumptions get challenged early. Features you thought were essential fall away. Problems you didn’t notice become obvious.

And that’s a good thing.

Collaboration reshapes the product

When you ship gradually and incrementally, in collaboration with real users, the product you end up with is almost never the one you originally imagined.

It’s usually smaller. More opinionated. More human.

It reflects tradeoffs, constraints, and lived experience — not just technical ideals.

That evolution isn’t a failure of vision. It’s proof that the work is grounded.

Build where the need is

If you want to help, start by finding people who are already struggling with a problem. Work with them directly. Let their needs shape the direction of what you build.

Software doesn’t matter because it’s clever. It matters because it’s used.

And the best way to make sure something gets used is to make sure it’s built with the people who need it — not handed to them fully formed from a distance.

That’s how the work stays meaningful.
